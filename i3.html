Amazon River is drying: What's going on with the largest river in the world?
The Amazon River basin is experiencing an unprecedented drought, with water levels plummeting to historic lows across the region. This environmental crisis is affecting navigation, wildlife, and local communities while raising alarm about the accelerating impacts of climate change.
About one-sixth of the world's freshwater that reaches the oceans passes through the Amazon's 320-km-wide delta as it empties into the Atlantic. The river’s size changes with the seasons. In the dry season, it spans 4 to 5 km in width, but during the wet season, this can swell to 50 km. At peak flow, the river's current can reach speeds of 7 kmph.
Further downstream in Tefe, a branch of the Solimoes has completely dried up, transforming once-navigable waterways into expanses of sand. The severity of the situation is highlighted by the state of Lake Tefe, where over 200 freshwater dolphins perished during last year's drought. The lake has now dried up entirely, depriving the endangered pink dolphins of a crucial habitat.Greenpeace spokesperson Romulo Batista emphasised the critical nature of this year's drought, noting that several months have broken last year's records. The drought's effects extend beyond the rivers, parching vegetation across Brazil and fueling wildfires that have engulfed South American cities in smoke. The drought's impact is particularly evident in Manaus, the Amazon's largest city. Here, where the Solimoes join the Rio Negro to form the Amazon River proper, water levels are approaching the record low set in October last year. Indigenous leader Kambeba told Reuters that the situation has worsened compared to the previous year, with drought conditions arriving earlier.
